This Financial Technology (FinTech) note proposes a next generation banking system architecture based on Blockchain Technology.

The urban Sec-A population are over-banked with multiple financial instruments across multiple delivery channels, even while 40% of the population are financially excluded.

The challenge for the next generation of FinTech innovators in India is to re-invent the banking system so that it is feasible to include the bottom of the pyramid financially excluded citizens of our Republic.

Both the authors, the Late Prof. C. K. Prahalad in his The Fortune at the Bottom of the Pyramid book, and Prof Abhijit V. Banerjee, in Poor Economics: A Radical Rethinking of the Way to Fight Global Poverty, have well documented how the poor consume in small amounts.

One can buy a mobile top up for Rs.8, shampoo sachet for Rs.3, a health drink sachet for Rs.5 and many more such products target the bottom of the pyramid.

Yet, to transfer Rs.10 between two bank accounts costs between Rs.2.5 to Rs.5 depending upon one’s financial status. The thumb rule banks follow is the more average balance you maintain less you pay for the transactions. This is counterproductive for the poor, where the 25% newly opened financial inclusive accounts have zero average balance.

While one can send peer to peer money between the same wallet at zero cost, using a Smart phone application, but then this happens in silos and certainly not interoperable.

Thus a few options exist for those who don’t have need to transfer small amounts; whereas the needy citizens are excluded from even this limited option.

Therefore, the challenge for the startups is to empower the newly included bottom of the pyramid consumers to make small payments electronically.

Can I buy the Rs.3 health drink sachet across the counter cashless without incurring a penalty? The answer is Yes! Mother Dairy has introduced a NFC (Near Field Communication) token where one can pay for small amounts, cashless. However, this solution while innovative is not portable to other brand stores.

New emerging technologies like NFC and Blockchain can provide a basis for disruptive startups to innovate in the FinTech space,relevant to India.

Introduction

In India we have a great divide between the haves and have nots. Here are some statistics, followed by some anecdotal episodes to support my point.

Statistics

While as per the RBI there were 36% adults included in banking system in 2009, and now various reports claim this figure to be around 60% inclusions in 2016. However, as per IMF report, in 2014 there were 1234 accounts per 1000 people in India. Also consider the fact that 219 million accounts were opened between 2014 and 2016 under the Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY) for the un-banked population.

Since the IMF data tallies with the World Bank data; the only explanation of the discrepancy between the 1234 accounts per 1000 people and the 40% population being excluded from the banking system, is that the urban Sec-A (or Top of the pyramid ) are over banked.

Anecdotal Evidence

I counted my family accounts which were 10 bank accounts for a family of 3 persons, excluding fixed deposit accounts. My neighbors and friends also reported a similar figure. Thus I conclude that my hypothesis of over banked Sec-A population to be holding ground.

While in our gated society we have ensured that all staffs of the society as well as household staffs are financially included by having a bank account in a scheduled bank; most of the household staffs and in-house casual service providers still prefer cash salaries.

Thus one of my co-investor of a startup, has devised a simple method, he creates a bonus scheme and pays the bonus to his household staff by on-line bank transfer, some of which he pays to their NPS (National Pension Scheme) accounts.

Why the State of the Art Core Banking Architecture is inadequate

As the banks are beings forced to get inclusive, and penetrating the bottom of the economic pyramid, they get challenged by their existing business model of subsidizing their high transactional cost with interest arbitrage earned from the float in their saving account products.

Even while the number of accounts increasing exponentially, the average balance is reducing rapidly. For example in the year 2009, when 36% households were financially included the average balance in savings bank deposits was Rs.7,767, in contrast, in the year 2016, 60% of the household are financially included but the average balance has dropped to Rs.2,333.

25% of the newly opened 219 million accounts between 2014 and 2016, under the Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY) have zero average balance.

Thus the Old school Ivy League Banks, with their high cost core banking model must transform or they will be a roadkill of the new age FinTech startups, moving rapidly with innovative technology fueled low cost business models.

Blockchain Technology

Blockchain concept refers to an open book, distributed ledger, where participants don’t need anyone’s permission to access the open ledger nor any permission is needed for transactions.

The participants don’t need to trust each other, the trust is based on mathematically secured algorithms. The transactions distributed across the networks are collated in batches and blocked together, then a consensus is reached among the participants, for transactions sequencing of these transactions with the block; Based upon algorithms for arriving at a consensus; verifiable by a "proof-of-work" or "proof-of-stake".

Each account of the participants are identified by a cryptographic hash of a Public Key, it’s corresponding Private Key is only needed to affect the transactions, and prove it’s authenticity and identity. Thus each participants can choose to be anonymous. These cryptographically secured blocks were chained to together, to make them immutable; Thereby it has the name: "block chain".

Bitcoin,the most popular cryptocurrency, was the first implementation of the Blockchain Technology. This was the version 1, in version 1.5 of Blockchain, startups like colored coins emerged, which enabled multiple currencies or tokens to ride upon Bitcoin. More, cryptocurrencies emerged and were traded over the Internet.

In Blockchain version 1.5, a combination trust based centralized wallets and currency exchanges emerged. This violation of basic idea of being a trust less, decentralized network, has resulted in mass scale frauds via breach of trust and large scale identity theft from & by these "trust" based centralized systems.

Cryptocurrencies have a high volatility as well as being anonymous in nature, illicit trading in drugs, terror funding and money laundering started to occur. Also, some of the large scale stealing and frauds occurred, where the centralized systems, like currency exchanges, were hacked and private keys stolen.

The lawmakers, with some help from the academia have been able understand the technology and have been able to track down and prosecute the block chain savvy criminals.

Also, the netizen have understood that they tend to loose anonymity as soon as they change the cryptocurrencies to fiat Currencies.

Around 2015, smart contract enabled Blockchain Technology 2.0 emerged with Ethereum, Interledger, Hyperledger, Nxt and Lighting Networks. These networks allow secured and distributed contracts applications.

It too has it’s share of frauds, Ethereum DAO (Distributed Autonomous Organization) Smart Contract, was probably the first widely reported fraud. It divided the community over the hard forking of the network. Since the Smart Contract performed exactly as specified, and Ethereum did not have any bugs, one section of the community broke away resulting in duplicated tokens.

However, the Ethereum itself survives the fork and so does Bitcoin in spite of multiple frauds in it’s numerous currency exchanges.

Regulatory Framework in India

India has a robust centralized payment system. NPCI (National Payment Corporation of India) a non-profit company formed by Indian Bankers' Association, facilitated by Reserve Bank of India (RBI) and IDRBT (Institute for Development and Research in Banking Technology) is running the Indian ATM Network, the Financial Transaction Switch, National Automated Clearing House, IMPS (Immediate Payment System, the Mobile payment system), AEPS (AADHAAR Enabled Payment System) Rupay (India’s answer to Visa & Master Card) and NEFT (National Electronic Fund Transfer).

Recently in April 2016, 10 banks launched their Mobile Application based on NPCI UPI (Unified Payment Interface) API. UPI, allows for Tokenization, and the UPI documents also mentions Blockchain networks in passing.

India has a strong and stable banking regulation, thanks to it’s regulator, Reserve Bank of India (RBI). To drive the financial inclusion, RBI has been opening up the banking space with various types of licenses.

Apart from the scheduled banks, the Urban and Rural Co-operative banks, it has allowed Prepaid Payment Instruments (Mobile Wallets), Payments banks and now Small Finance Banks and On Tap Banking licenses. Also, it has allowed formation of private entities to act as Banking Business Correspondent, who can extend the banking outreach beyond bank’s branches.

Why So Many Licenses?

Money is an artificial device invented for measuring, storing and exchanging of economic value created by the society.

Storage of Value requires Security and Stability; While Exchange of value requires agility and rapid paced innovation. For the scheduled banks balancing act between Stability and Agility has resulted in slow paced innovation. Thus, RBI have enabled multiple types of licenses to address these diverse requirements.

Different licenses provide coverage for different aspects and use-cases of payments and banking needs of the Indian citizen.

Adapting the Blockchain Technology for FinTech Adoption

Bank of England, EU Central Bank, the US Regulators as well as RBI have formed committees to look into Blockchain and have notified or published Blockchain position papers.

Blockchain 2.0, with Smart Contract beholds the promise to revolutionize and disrupt the high costs of banking and slow pace of innovation in FinTech.

Some of the forward looking banks have been experimenting with Blockchain technology mainly around cross border money transfers, and financial Supply Chain.

Indian Context

With RBI allowing Payments Banks, Prepaid Instrument, on-tap banking licenses, along with publishing the UPI specification; we can now innovate with NFC (Near Field Communication) Tokens, and Blockchain enabled smart contracts.

Blockchain 2.0 Network allows for Public, Private and Consortium (permission) Based Network. These three type of Blockchain Networks correspond to Internet, LAN and VPN in TCP/IP parlance.

Using Blockchain Network, with UPI API and NFC we can enable multiple use cases like low value high velocity money transfer between citizens.

I envisage a consortium based Blockchain network, facilitated by NPCI that will replace the today’s high cost core banking infrastructure with low cost, secured and transparent distributed ledger.

This will also enable innovative features, like Account Portability.

Like we have the MNP (Mobile Number Interoperability) between Telecommunication Networks, Bank Account Portability with automatic balance and ledger transfer will be possible with Blockchain. Thus we will not loose the transaction histories when we change our banking service providers. This will foster health competition among banks and reduce transaction costs.

Regulator can conduct real-time audit and AML (Anti Money Laundering) and Combating of Financing of Terrorism (CFT) monitoring. Reducing regulatory burden on the banks on one hand and improving the regulator’s visibility into the money supply and fund flow on the other hand.

Today, 15 August 2016, being India’s 70th Independence Day, I envision our Freedom from the high transaction charges in next few years.
